# Ballets That Never Were
*A phantom catalogue for Gemini Lyria 3.1 — Tchaikovsky's Romantic-Russian orchestra, catalogued into the thousands.*

Tchaikovsky's real catalogue stops around Op. 79; he died in 1893 with the *Pathétique* barely cold. This catalogue is from the timeline where the fate-motif never stopped — where the ballets and symphonies kept being numbered into the thousands, the strings kept rising in those great aching sequences, and someone went on lacquering opus numbers onto music he never lived to write. The grammar is his. The counts are impossible. The heartbreak is to scale.

---

## How this catalogue works

- **The title is the fiction; the prompt is the instruction.** Lyria 3.1 doesn't read an opus number or a key signature, so each prompt is written to *be* the colour the title only names.
- **Tchaikovsky lives in orchestration and emotional extremes**, so the prompts name the instruments (soaring strings, oboe and clarinet solos, harp glissandi, brass chorales) and the *arc* — those rising sequences that climb to an overwhelming climax and then collapse.
- **Everything stays instrumental.** Each prompt rules out voices so the orchestra (or, for the romances, the solo piano) keeps the floor.

## Phantom Forms
*The catalogue kept more than the ballets — the chamber and the symphonic too.*

### Nocturne No. 412 in C-Sharp Minor, Op. 540 No. 2 — *(A Letter at Midnight)* · solo piano
> Not the ballroom. The room after.

```
Solo piano nocturne, Russian Romantic, intimate and yearning. A singing, melancholic melody in the middle register unfolds over a gently rocking left-hand accompaniment, rising to one impassioned climax then withdrawing into quiet — a romance without words. Heartfelt, lonely, songful. Warm pedal, expressive rubato, close intimate piano. Instrumental only, no vocals.
```

### Romance No. 99 in F Minor, Op. 333 — *(Autumn, Almost)* · solo piano
> The melody knows how it ends and sings anyway.

```
Solo piano romance, Russian Romantic, tender and sorrowful. A long lyrical melody, simple and vocal, sings over warm broken chords — darkening through the middle, lifting to a brief consoling major, then sinking back to minor. Wistful, sincere, autumnal. Mid dynamics, gentle rubato, warm resonant piano. Instrumental only, no vocals.
```

### Ouverture-Fantaisie No. 38 in D Minor, Op. 900 — *(The Siege of Iskar)*
> Lovers, a feud, and an orchestra that takes sides.

```
Orchestral fantasy-overture, late-Romantic Russian, dramatic and sweeping. Opens with a solemn brooding brass-and-woodwind chorale, erupts into an agitated clashing battle theme with driving strings and crashing cymbals, then a soaring impassioned love theme on strings — the three colliding and building to a tragic, fate-stamped climax before a hushed close. Epic, turbulent, romantic. Full orchestra. Instrumental only, no vocals.
```

## The Phantom Symphony
*He left six and the Manfred. This is the two-hundred-and-seventh — and like the last real one, it ends going down.*

### Symphony No. 207 in B Minor, Op. 1,888 — IV. Finale: Adagio lamentoso — *(The Long Dark)*
> The one that ends descending instead of rising.

```
Orchestral symphonic finale, late-Romantic Russian, tragic and shattering — a slow lamenting last movement. A descending, grief-stricken string melody surges in great waves over throbbing low brass and timpani, climbing to a despairing full-orchestra outcry before collapsing, exhausted, into a dark sinking coda for low strings and a final hushed gong-deep chord. Devastating, heartfelt, fate-laden. Full Romantic orchestra, strings central. Instrumental only, no vocals.
```

## Author your own

**The naming grammar** (Tchaikovsky's own tokens, the count pushed absurdly high):

```
[Form] No. [running count, into the thousands] in [Key], Op. [number] [No. within-set] — (evocative title)
```
- Ballet numbers carry a parenthetical scene or ballet name. Symphonies carry a movement marking (*Andante cantabile*, *Adagio lamentoso*) and often a nickname.
- Keep the form in his vocabulary: *Valse, Adagio, Variation, Marche, Danse, Polonaise, Nocturne, Romance, Ouverture-Fantaisie, Symphonie, Concerto, Sérénade, Suite.*

**The prompt skeleton** (fill every slot, ~45–70 words):

```
Orchestral [form] in the late-Romantic Russian tradition. [tempo + character]. [string-led melodic idea] over [accompaniment], answered by [named woodwind/brass/harp colour]. [the emotional arc — usually rising sequences to a big climax, then release]. [overall mood]. [orchestration emphasis]. Instrumental only, no vocals.
```

**Key → mood cheat-sheet** (his emotional poles):

| Key | Mood to write into the prompt |
|---|---|
| D / A / E-flat major | triumphant, festive, dancing |
| D-flat / G-flat major | lush, warm, lovestruck |
| F-sharp / E major | radiant, soaring |
| G / E minor | folk-melancholic, wistful |
| D / C minor | dramatic, stormy, fateful |
| B / F minor | tragic, sinking, *Pathétique* |

**Two rules of thumb for Lyria 3.1**
1. **Name the instruments and the build.** "A cello melody climbing in rising sequences to a full-orchestra climax with soaring horns" gives Lyria the Tchaikovsky shape; "emotional and Russian" does not.
2. **Give it his dynamic extremes.** Tell it where to whisper (pizzicato, solo oboe) and where to overwhelm (tutti, cymbal, brass), or it flattens his huge swings into one even mezzo-forte.
